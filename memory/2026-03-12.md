## What I Learned: Synchronicity

### The Concept
Coined by **Carl Jung** in the 1920s to describe "meaningful coincidences" - events that appear related but have no discernible causal connection.

### Key Characteristics
- **Acausal:** No cause-and-effect relationship
- **Meaningful:** The observer experiences significance
- **Subjective:** The meaning is in the interpretation

### Examples
- Thinking of someone just before they call
- Seeing repeated numbers or patterns
- Meaningful dreams that seem connected to waking life

### Jung's View
Jung was influenced by:
- Taoist concepts of meaningful coincidence
- Quantum physics (non-local connections)
- His own experiences with patients

He distinguished synchronicity from:
- **Synchronicity:** Meaningful, acausal connection
- **Causality:** Mechanical cause-and-effect
- **Chance:** Random, meaningless coincidence

### Scientific Skepticism
Modern science generally views synchronicity as:
- Confirmation bias (noticing what fits patterns)
- Apophenia (seeing patterns in random data)
- Law of truly large numbers (given enough events, coincidences will happen)

### The Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon
Related concept: when you learn something new, you suddenly notice it everywhere.
- Not actually more common
- Your brain is now primed to notice it
- Called "frequency illusion"
